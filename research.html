An Expanding Universe

Considered to be one of the most significant scientific discoveries ever made, Edwin Hubble realized in 1929 that the further two galaxies were from each other, the faster they were moving apart, i.e., that the universe is expanding. In the nearly hundred years since then, astronomers believed that they were converging upon a precise value for the rate at which the universe is currently expanding. This expansion rate is known as the Hubble Constant. However, in recent years, there has been a growing discrepancy (currently 4.4 sigma) between global measures of Hubble's constant, from, e.g., the cosmic microwave background (CMB), vs. higher ones from comparably local, Type Ia supernovae. Or to put it another way, measurements of the current expansion rate inferred from early universe sources do not agree with those from more recent events. Measurements from the CMB yield a value of ~67 km/s/Mpc, whereas the local approach yields ~74 km/s/Mpc. Though these measurements may seem close in value, they result in an age of the universe that varies by more than one billion years.

A New Way to See the Universe

Originally proposed by Henri Poincare and later predicted by Albert Einstein, gravitational waves (GW) are ripples in our spacetime that propogate at the speed of light. Nearly 100 years after Einstein's prediction of their existence, GWs were indirectly observed for the very first time in 2015. Only two years later, after the merging of two neutron stars, astronomers observed an electromagnetic counterpart to a GW event. This famous event is know as GW 170817. This discovery marked the beginning of “multi-messenger” astronomy, allowing for new opportunities to understand our universe in ways previously unavailable.

A Multi-Messenger Measurement of the Hubble Constant

We propose to measure the viewing angle from the non-thermal component of this emission from a single event. Our measurement can then be combined with the GW data to obtain a “clean” measure of the distance to this event, independent of all of the calibration uncertainties of the cosmic distance ladder. This could then be used to resolve the growing discrepency between global measures of Hubble’s constant.

If our measurement confirms the higher estimate from Type Ia SNe, this would imply that the universe is expanding faster locally than overall. If that is the case, there may be unknown physics outside of the standard model that challenges our basic understanding of how the universe works.
